## Step 1: Claim and Verify Your Google Business Profile

Google Business Profile (GBP) — previously called Google My Business — is a free listing that controls how your business appears in Google Search and Google Maps.

If you haven't set yours up yet, start here: **business.google.com**

**To create or claim your profile:**
1. Go to business.google.com and sign in with a Google account
2. Search for your business name — if it already exists in Google's database, claim it; if not, create it from scratch
3. Enter your business name, category, and location
4. Google will ask you to verify — usually by postcard sent to your business address (takes 5–10 days), or sometimes by phone, video, or email depending on your business type

**Common mistake:** Using a personal Gmail rather than a business email. Set up a business email (you@yourbusiness.co.uk) before you start — it looks more professional and keeps things organised.

---

## Step 2: Fill In Every Section Completely

Once verified, most business owners stop at the basics — name, address, phone number. Don't. Google rewards completeness. Here's everything you should fill in:

**Business name:** Use your actual business name. Don't stuff keywords in here ("John's Plumber Dumfries Best Value") — Google penalises this and it looks amateur.

**Category:** Choose the most specific primary category that describes what you do. Add secondary categories where relevant. For example, a B&B might have "Bed and Breakfast" as primary and "Guest House" as secondary.

**Address:** Make sure this matches exactly how your address appears on your website and any other listings (Royal Mail format). Inconsistency confuses Google.

**Phone number:** Use a local dialling code if possible (01387, 01556, 01776, etc.). It signals you're genuinely local.

**Website:** Link to your website. If you don't have one yet, this is another reason to get one — GBP listings with website links perform significantly better.

**Opening hours:** Keep these accurate. Out-of-date hours that show you as closed when you're open (or vice versa) damage trust and cost you customers. Add special hours for bank holidays and seasonal closures.

**Business description:** You get 750 characters. Write naturally about what you do, who you serve, and what makes you worth choosing. Mention your location — "based in Dumfries," "serving Galloway and the surrounding area" — this helps local search relevance.

**Services and products:** Add these specifically. A plumber might list boiler servicing, emergency callouts, bathroom fitting. A restaurant adds menu items. These become searchable.

**Attributes:** Depending on your category, Google offers specific attributes — "wheelchair accessible," "free Wi-Fi," "dog friendly," "outdoor seating." Fill in all that apply. These match search filters people use.

## Step 3: Add Photos (Properly)

Google's own research shows that businesses with photos on their GBP listing receive 42% more requests for directions and 35% more website visits than those without.

**What to upload:**
- Your logo and a cover photo (used in the Google Maps listing)
- Your premises — exterior and interior
- Your team at work (particularly powerful for tradespeople and hospitality)
- Products, menu items, completed jobs
- Any events or seasonal imagery

**Practical tips:**
- Upload photos at least 720×720 pixels
- Use your real business photos, not stock images — authenticity builds trust
- Update photos seasonally — Google favours active profiles
- Do NOT use photos with promotional text overlaid (Google filters these out)

Aim for at least 10 photos to start. The more active and complete your profile, the better it ranks.

---

## Step 4: Get Reviews — and Respond to All of Them

Reviews are one of the most powerful signals in local search. More reviews, more recent reviews, and higher average ratings all improve your position in Google Maps results.

**How to get reviews:**
- Ask directly, immediately after a job or visit: "If you're happy with the work, it would mean a lot if you left us a Google review — it really helps a small local business."
- Send a text or email after a booking with a direct link to your GBP review page (Google provides a short link you can copy from your profile dashboard)
- Add a "Leave us a review" link to your website and email footer
- For hospitality businesses, a small card at checkout with a QR code works extremely well

**The single most important thing most D&G businesses aren't doing:** responding to every review — positive and negative.

Responding to positive reviews shows you're engaged. It also gives you a natural opportunity to include location and service keywords ("Thanks for visiting us here in Thornhill — we're glad the full bathroom installation went smoothly").

Responding to negative reviews professionally demonstrates to future customers that you take complaints seriously and handle them like an adult. Never argue, never dismiss. Acknowledge, apologise where appropriate, offer to resolve offline.

---

## Step 5: Post Updates Regularly

Google Business Profile has a Posts feature — like a very simple social media feed that appears in your listing. Most businesses in D&G ignore this entirely.

Use it. Post:
- New services or seasonal offerings
- Special opening hours or closures
- Local events you're involved in
- Photos of recent completed work
- Promotions (for retail, food & drink)

Posts have a shelf life of about a week before they're less visible, so aim to post once a week or once a fortnight. It takes five minutes. Each post is another signal to Google that your profile is active and current.

---

## Step 6: Your Website — The Foundation Under All of This

Here's something important: Google Business Profile alone won't get you to the top of local search. It works best when it's connected to a website that reinforces the same signals.

A website helps local SEO by:
- Confirming your business name, address, and phone number consistently (what SEOs call NAP consistency)
- Providing pages specifically targeting location-based keywords — "plumber in Lockerbie," "B&B near Galloway Forest Park"
- Earning links from other local websites (your Chamber of Commerce listing, VisitScotland, local directories)
- Providing content — blog posts, service descriptions — that tells Google what you do and where

If your business doesn't have a website, or has one that doesn't include your location and services clearly, it's like building a beautiful shop window with no address on the door.

---

## Step 7: Get Listed in Local Directories

Consistency matters in local SEO. Make sure your business name, address, and phone number appears identically across:

- **Google Business Profile** (most important)
- **Apple Maps** (claimed via Apple Business Connect — apple.com/uk/business-connect)
- **Bing Places** (bingplaces.com — still used, especially on older devices)
- **Yell.com** (well-indexed by Google)
- **Scoot.co.uk**
- **Thomson Local**
- **VisitScotland** (essential for tourism businesses)
- **D&G Chamber of Commerce** listing if you're a member

You don't need to be on hundreds of directories — just make sure the major ones are accurate and consistent.
