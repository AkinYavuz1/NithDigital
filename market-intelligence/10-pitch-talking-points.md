# Pitch Talking Points — Face-to-Face & Phone Reference

## 1. How to Introduce Nith Digital

**Don't say:** "I run a web design company and I'd like to tell you about our services."

**Do say something like:**

> "I'm a developer based in Dumfries — I work with local businesses to help them get found on Google and actually convert visitors into customers. I've been doing data and digital work for about 10 years and I got fed up seeing great D&G businesses invisible online, so I started Nith Digital."

> "I was actually looking up [type of business] in [area] recently and your name came up — I noticed a few things on your site that I thought could be costing you customers, and I just wanted to mention it."

> "I do two things — I build websites for local businesses, and I do data analytics work for larger clients. The websites are my way of giving something back to D&G, to be honest. I see too many good businesses being let down by their digital presence."

**Key principle:** Lead with *their* problem, not your product. You noticed something about *them*. That's what opens doors.

---

## 2. Discovery Questions

Ask at least 4–5 of these before pitching anything. The answers will shape which solution you lead with and which pain points to emphasise.

1. "How do most of your new customers find you at the moment — is it mostly word of mouth, or do you get people coming through Google?"
2. "Do you have a website at the moment? When was it last updated?" *(If they don't know, that tells you everything.)*
3. "Have you ever looked at your website on a phone? How do you think it comes across?" *(Let them discover the problem themselves.)*
4. "Are you on Booking.com / Airbnb / third-party platforms?" *(For hospitality.) "What do they charge you per booking?"*
5. "Do you get enquiries through your website, or is it mostly the phone?"
6. "Has anyone ever told you they couldn't find you online, or struggled to get in touch?"
7. "Is there a particular type of job or customer you'd love more of?" *(This shapes the SEO and content angle.)*
8. "What does your busiest day of the week look like? And your quietest?" *(For salons, restaurants — the quieter days are the problem the website solves.)*

**Listen for:** anything that signals frustration with their current digital situation. That's your opening.

## 3. Key Stats to Drop into Conversation

Use these naturally — not as a lecture, as a prompt for their own reflection.

### The Big Three (universal)
- **"80% of customers Google a business before visiting."**  
  *Use when:* the business says they rely on word of mouth.  
  *Say:* "Even when someone gets a recommendation from a friend, 80% of them still Google it before they go."

- **"46% of all Google searches are local."**  
  *Use when:* they think Google is for big companies.  
  *Say:* "Nearly half of everything people search on Google has a local intent — 'plumber near me', 'restaurant [town]'. That's your customers."

- **"53% of mobile users leave a site if it takes more than 3 seconds to load."**  
  *Use when:* they have an old site.  
  *Say:* "So if someone Googles you on their phone and your site takes 5 seconds to load, more than half of them are gone before they even see you."

### D&G-Specific Stats
- **"D&G has about 6,300 businesses — 88% of them are micro businesses without a proper digital team."**  
  *Use when:* they think everyone else is already doing this well.  
  *Say:* "The competition online in D&G is actually quite low compared to cities. If you have a good website and a bit of SEO, you can be the top result in your area relatively quickly."

- **"D&G tourism is worth £158 million a year — 520,000 overnight trips — and most visitors are searching on their phones before they travel."**  
  *Use when:* pitching to hospitality or activity businesses.

- **"34% of company directors in D&G are over 60 — a lot of the local competition is running websites from 10+ years ago."**  
  *Use when:* they're worried about competing with established players.  
  *Say:* "Honestly, a lot of your competitors in D&G still have websites from 2012. A modern site stands out immediately."

- **"Tourism businesses in D&G are paying 15–25% commission on third-party platforms. That's £150–£250 on a single £1,000 booking."**  
  *Use for:* hotels, B&Bs, self-catering operators.

## 4. How to Present Pricing Without Scaring Them Off

### The Anchor Principle
Always mention a higher figure first, so the actual price feels reasonable by comparison.

**Script:**
> "A full custom website build for a business like yours — something a bigger agency in Glasgow would charge for — typically runs £2,800 to £5,000. We can do the same result starting from £500, because we use purpose-built templates for D&G businesses that we've already done the design work on. You get a proper professional site, just without the custom agency fee."

### The Investment Framing
Never say "cost" — say "investment". And tie it to a return:

> "For a B&B, if we get you two extra direct bookings a month instead of Booking.com bookings, you've saved £200–£300 in commission. The website pays for itself in three to four months."

> "For a tradesperson, if the website gets you one extra job a month — say a bathroom installation — you've paid for the website in the first week."

### The Payment Plan Option
If they wince at the price, offer to spread it:

> "We can split it into two payments — half upfront, half when the site goes live. That's about [£X] now to get started."

### Price Points to Use Verbally
- "Websites start from £500" *(this is the door-opener — gets attention)*
- "A typical business website for a [trade/B&B/restaurant] is around £800–£1,200"
- "If you want the full works — custom design, booking integration, SEO setup — we're talking £1,500–£2,500"
- Monthly retainer: "From £75 a month we handle hosting, updates, and keep an eye on your Google presence"

## 5. Handling Objections

---

### "I already have a Facebook page"

**Don't argue. Validate, then reframe:**

> "Facebook is great for keeping existing customers updated and building a community. But it won't help someone who's searching 'plumber Dumfries' on Google at 9pm on a Sunday — because they're not on your Facebook page, they're in a search result. You need both, really, but the website is what gets you in front of people who don't already know you."

---

### "My nephew does my website"

**Avoid making them feel bad about the nephew:**

> "That's great that you've got someone helping — it obviously saves money. The question is just whether the site is actually ranking on Google and converting visitors. A lot of sites that are built by someone close to the business are technically fine but haven't got the SEO or the structure to appear in search results. If your nephew's happy to collaborate, I can work alongside him — I often provide the strategy and SEO layer and the business owner handles the content updates."

*(Alternative if the site is genuinely bad):*  
> "Can I ask — when did it last get updated? And have you ever checked how it looks on a mobile phone?"

---

### "I can't afford it right now"

**Acknowledge, then find the real objection:**

> "That's fair — I'm not here to pressure anyone into something they're not ready for. Can I ask, is it more the upfront cost that's the issue, or is it that you're not sure whether you'd get the return on it?"

- If it's the upfront cost: offer the payment plan option (above).
- If it's the ROI concern: go back to the stats. One extra booking, one extra job, one extra table booking per week is often enough to cover the cost many times over.
- If timing is genuinely bad: "When would be a better time to chat? Can I follow up in a couple of months?"

---

### "I don't need a website"

**This one needs a direct but friendly challenge:**

> "I hear that a lot, and I always ask the same question: how do you know? Because if someone searched for you last week and couldn't find you, you'd never know about it — they'd have just called someone else. The customers you're missing are invisible to you."

> "Can I show you something? [Open phone.] Search '[their type of business] [their town]' on Google. Are you in those results? No? That's business that's going to someone else right now."

---

### "I'll think about it"

**This usually means they need one more reason or they're not the decision-maker:**

> "Of course, take your time. Can I ask — is there a specific part of it you want to think through? Sometimes it helps to talk through any concerns before you decide."

If they're the decision-maker and still hesitant:

> "What if I put together a specific proposal for you — not a generic quote, but a plan written for [their business name] with suggested content and a price? That way you've got something concrete to think about. I can have it with you in 48 hours."

The proposal creates momentum and gives them something tangible to agree to.

---

### "I'm tied into a contract with someone else"

**Don't compete with the contract — compete with the results:**

> "Completely understand. When does that run until? The reason I ask is that a lot of the businesses I talk to aren't actually unhappy with their current provider — they just aren't seeing any return from their website. If it's working well for you, that's great. If it's not generating enquiries, it might be worth reviewing when the contract's up."

> "I'm happy to just stay in touch and come back to you nearer the end of the contract. What I can do in the meantime is send over a free audit of your current site — no obligation — so you can see exactly what's working and what isn't. That way you'll have something concrete to compare any future proposals against."

## 6. Closing: How to Move to Next Steps

**The goal of every meeting or call is one of three things:**
1. A signed proposal / agreement
2. A specific follow-up date ("Can I come back to you on the 15th?")
3. A concrete next step ("Let me send you a proposal and you can have a look")

**Never leave a meeting without one of these.**

---

### Soft close (if they seem interested but not yet ready):

> "Look, I'm not going to push you into anything. What I'd suggest is this — let me put together a quick proposal specific to [business name] with a price and a plan, and you can take a few days to look at it. No pressure, no invoice until you say go. Sound fair?"

---

### Direct close (if the meeting has gone well and they're clearly keen):

> "So based on what you've told me, I'd recommend [package] — that would cost [price], and we'd have it live in about [timescale]. Should I send over a proposal today and we can get started?"

---

### The next-step close (for prospects who need more time):

> "I'll send you the demo link with your business name on it — takes two minutes to look at. Have a read and just let me know if any questions come up. Would next [day] work for a quick follow-up call?"

---

### One-liner closing phrases that work:

- "The only question is: do you want to be getting enquiries from Google in 4 weeks, or not?"
- "I can have this live before the summer tourist season. That's worth something."
- "Most of my clients say they wish they'd done it sooner. The only thing stopping you now is the decision."
- "We can start with the starter package — and if it's working in 3 months, we can build on it."
