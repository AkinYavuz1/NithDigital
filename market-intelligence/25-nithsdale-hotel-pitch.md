## Section 1: Situation Analysis — What They Have Now

### Website Platform
The Nithsdale Hotel website (nithsdalehotel.com) is built on **Amenitiz** — a SaaS platform aimed at independent hotels that bundles website building, a booking engine, and channel management into one product. Amenitiz is a functional but generic tool; it was clearly chosen because it promised to solve the booking problem without technical complexity.

### What the Current Website Looks Like
The site has the typical characteristics of an Amenitiz build:
- Template-based design with limited customisation options
- Standard page structure: Home, Rooms, Restaurant, Menu, Contact, Booking
- Built-in booking widget (Amenitiz's native engine) — so technically they do have online booking
- An online ordering subdomain exists at online.nithsdalehotel.com (Sitin integration for eat-in menus)
- Legal notices page suggests basic compliance is in place

### What Is Wrong With It

**Design and brand limitations:** Amenitiz templates are functional but visually bland and immediately recognisable as a platform template rather than a designed website. The hotel has genuine character — a historic high street building, a log-fire bar, a well-reviewed restaurant, a dog-friendly culture — but the website likely fails to convey any of that atmosphere. Guests searching online form their impression from the website before they ever visit; a generic template undersells a property with real warmth.

**SEO invisibility:** The biggest problem with Amenitiz for a small UK hotel is SEO. The platform is not built with UK local SEO in mind. A search for "hotel Sanquhar", "accommodation Upper Nithsdale", or "hotel near Sanquhar" returns OTA listings (Booking.com, TripAdvisor, Expedia) before the hotel's own website. The hotel is likely paying Booking.com commissions of 15–18% on every booking that arrives via OTA — money that a well-optimised direct website would keep in their pocket.

**OTA dependency:** The hotel is listed on Booking.com, TripAdvisor, Expedia, Hotels.com, KAYAK, and Lodging World. This is good for visibility but creates a structural problem: every OTA booking costs them money, and the OTAs are also harvesting their guests' email addresses, meaning the hotel cannot build a direct marketing list from guests who book via platform.

**Social media vs website disconnect:** Their Instagram (@nithsdalehotel, 334 posts) and Facebook page (5,473 likes) are active and generate genuine local engagement. But social media profiles almost certainly direct interested guests to OTA booking links or a generic contact form rather than a compelling direct booking journey. The social media effort is benefiting Booking.com more than it benefits the hotel.

**Google Business Profile:** Almost certainly claimed (a property this well-reviewed on TripAdvisor and Booking.com will have received Google's prompts to claim), but the quality of the GBP optimisation is unknown. Given that the website is an Amenitiz template, it is unlikely that the hotel has done any structured work on their GBP — keyword-rich description, Q&A responses, post cadence, photo optimisation, or service listings. This is low-hanging fruit.

**Room rate context:** Rack rates from around £47/night on third-party sites, with room pricing influenced by day of week and season. Average room rate is likely in the £65–£90 range at typical occupancy. With 13 rooms, the revenue potential from converting even modest OTA bookings to direct bookings is meaningful.

## Section 5: ROI Argument

This is the most important section for closing the sale. Frame the conversation around the commission bleed, not the website cost.

### The Commission Calculation

Assuming 13 rooms, 50% average annual occupancy (conservative for a 3-star Visit Scotland rated hotel), at an average rate of £70/night:

- 13 rooms x 365 nights x 50% occupancy x £70 = **£166,075 annual room revenue**
- If 60% of bookings currently come via OTAs at 17% average commission: 60% of £166,075 = £99,645 via OTA
- Commission cost: £99,645 x 17% = **~£16,940/year going to Booking.com and Expedia**

A website that shifts even 20% of those OTA bookings to direct (entirely achievable with proper SEO and a compelling direct booking offer) would save:
- 20% of £99,645 = £19,929 redirected to direct
- Commission saved: £19,929 x 17% = **~£3,389/year**

The website pays for itself in under 5 months on commission savings alone — before accounting for any increase in overall bookings from improved SEO.

### The Direct Booking Conservative Argument

Even on the simplest framing: if the new website generates just **2 additional bookings per month at £75/night average**, that is:
- 2 x £75 x 12 = **£1,800/year in new revenue**
- Against a website cost of £1,250 = **ROI achieved in Year 1**

And that is new revenue, not just saved commission. The care plan cost of £1,140/year is more than covered by the commission savings alone.

### The Guest Data Value

Every direct booking captures a name and email address that the hotel owns. OTA bookings do not. Over 2 years, 200–300 direct booking email addresses enable seasonal campaigns, last-minute availability fills, and repeat booking incentives. This guest relationship data has long-term compounding value that is difficult to price but genuinely significant for a small owner-operated property.

## Section 8: Objection Handling

**Response:** "You do — and it's functional, which is a good start. When someone Googles 'hotel near Sanquhar' or 'accommodation Nithsdale valley', they're finding Booking.com and TripAdvisor, not your own site. That's the booking commission problem."

### "We get enough bookings through Booking.com and TripAdvisor"
**Response:** "I hear that a lot, and I understand — those platforms definitely deliver bookings. The question is the cost. If you're doing reasonable occupancy and 60% is coming through OTAs at 17% commission, you're probably spending over £10,000 a year in fees. A website that shifts even a quarter of that to direct would pay for itself several times over in Year 1. The OTAs will always be part of the mix — but ideally as a marketing channel that introduces guests, rather than the primary booking route."
