# 5 Signs Your Business Website Needs Replacing

**Meta title:** 5 Signs Your Business Website Needs Replacing
**Meta description:** Is your website quietly costing you customers? These five signs mean it's time for a new one — with real examples from D&G businesses.
**Target keyword:** when to replace business website
**Word count target:** 1,000–1,500 words

---

Most business owners don't wake up one day and decide their website is failing them. It happens gradually. The site was built years ago, it still sort of works, and there are always more pressing things to deal with. The website gets pushed to the bottom of the list.

The trouble is, a website that's quietly underperforming is quietly losing you business every single day. Potential customers land on it, decide you don't look credible, and move on to a competitor. You never know about it — those visitors just disappear without picking up the phone.

Here are five clear signs it's time to replace your business website. If you recognise more than one of these, the cost of doing nothing is almost certainly higher than the cost of fixing it.

---

## 1. It Doesn't Work Properly on a Mobile Phone

This is the most common and most damaging problem in D&G right now.

Over 60% of web searches happen on a mobile phone. For local searches specifically — someone looking for a tradesperson, a place to eat, or somewhere to stay while they're already in the area — that figure is even higher, closer to 70–80%.

If your website isn't mobile-friendly, that means the majority of your potential customers are landing on a site where they have to pinch and zoom to read text, the buttons are too small to tap, and the phone number is buried or formatted as an image they can't click. Most of them won't bother. They'll go back to Google and click the next result.

A plumber we spoke to in Sanquhar had a website that looked fine on a laptop but was essentially unusable on a phone. He was getting a trickle of enquiries compared to a competitor in Dumfries who had a newer, mobile-first site. The difference wasn't skills or pricing — it was that one of them was reachable on a phone and one wasn't.

Google also penalises non-mobile-friendly sites in its search rankings. So not only do you lose the visitors who land on a bad mobile experience — you also rank lower and get fewer visitors in the first place.

**Quick test:** Open your website on your phone right now. Can you read the text without zooming? Can you tap the phone number to call? Is the menu usable? If any of those are a no, you have a problem.

---

## 2. It Loads Slowly

Attention spans online are short. Research consistently shows that if a web page takes more than 3 seconds to load, over half of visitors will leave before they've seen anything. On mobile connections, which are common in rural areas of D&G, even a marginally slow site can be devastating for your bounce rate.

Slow websites are usually caused by old code, unoptimised images, outdated hosting, or simply a website builder platform that was never built for performance. Sites built on Weebly, old versions of Joomla, or early WordPress themes with dozens of plugins loading in the background are common culprits.

We audited the website of a holiday cottage business near Kirkcudbright last year. Their site was taking 11 seconds to load on mobile. That's not a slight inconvenience — that's a business losing potential bookings every single day to competitors whose sites are clean and fast.

Page speed is also a Google ranking factor. Faster sites rank higher. Slower sites don't just lose the visitors who leave early — they also struggle to attract visitors in the first place.

**Quick test:** Type your website URL into [Google PageSpeed Insights](https://pagespeed.web.dev) and see your score. Under 50 is poor. Under 70 on mobile means there's real room to improve. A score under 40 is urgent.

---

## 3. It Looks Dated or Untrustworthy

This one is harder to self-diagnose because we tend to become blind to things we see every day. But first impressions matter enormously online, and they happen in milliseconds.

Research from Stanford found that 75% of people judge a company's credibility based on its website design. If your site has small, blurry photos, cluttered layouts, fonts from the early 2010s, or stock imagery that looks obviously fake, visitors draw conclusions — consciously or not — about whether you're a credible, trustworthy business.

A guest house owner in Castle Douglas told us she'd had guests say they nearly didn't book because her website "looked old." She was running a beautifully maintained property — but her website made it look like something that hadn't been touched since 2011. Which, as it turned out, it hadn't.

An outdated website doesn't just look bad — it communicates that you might not pay much attention to details, that you might not be in business any more, that you might not be on top of things. For a B&B, a restaurant, or a tradesperson where trust is everything, that's a serious problem.

**The bar has risen.** What looked acceptable five years ago now looks genuinely dated. If your website was built before 2020 and hasn't been refreshed since, it's worth being honest with yourself about whether it reflects the quality of your work.

---

## 4. You Can't Update It Yourself

If you need to call a developer every time you want to change your opening hours, update a price, add a new service, or post a photo of a recent job — your website is working against you.

A website you can't update is a website that gradually becomes inaccurate and stale. We've seen sites in D&G advertising Christmas opening hours in March. Restaurant menus from two years ago. "Coming soon" pages that never got finished. Testimonials from 2018.

All of those things tell a potential customer that nobody's home.

Modern websites — whether built on WordPress, a decent CMS, or a modern framework — should let a non-technical business owner make basic updates easily. Change a price, swap a photo, add an announcement about a new service, post a short news item. It should take five minutes, not a phone call and a wait.

If your current website doesn't give you that ability, the next time you rebuild it, make sure it does. Ask specifically: "Can I log in and edit the content myself?" and "What does that look like?" Ask for a demo before you sign anything.

---

## 5. It's Not Appearing on Google

The whole point of a website is that people can find you. If you can't be found, you might as well not have one.

Search "your trade + your town" right now. Where do you appear? If you're not on the first page of Google — ideally in the top five results — you're not getting meaningful organic traffic. And if your competitors are appearing above you, they're getting the calls and enquiries that should be going to you.

Poor Google visibility usually comes down to a few fixable things: no local SEO on the site, no Google Business Profile, the site structure doesn't tell Google clearly what you do and where, or the site is so old and slow that Google doesn't rank it well.

A landscaping business in Thornhill had a website — they'd had it for years — but it appeared on page 4 of Google for "landscaper Thornhill." Nobody searches page 4. Effectively, they had no digital presence at all. Their competitor, a newer business with a straightforward WordPress site and a complete Google Business Profile, was appearing first — and they'd been in business for less time.

## What to Do Next

If you recognised yourself in two or more of these, it's worth getting a proper assessment of your current site before you decide what to do about it. Sometimes a partial fix is possible. Often a clean rebuild is the faster and more cost-effective answer.

The good news is that a new website doesn't have to be complicated or expensive. A focused, well-built 5-page site with fast loading, good mobile design, and basic local SEO will outperform a bloated, outdated site every time.
